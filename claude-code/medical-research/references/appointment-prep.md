# Appointment Prep Scaffold

## Before the Appointment

### Build Your Question List

You likely have 10–20 minutes. Prioritize ruthlessly.

**Must-answer (3–5 questions max):**
- The question you came specifically to have answered
- What happens if we do nothing? (watchful waiting / natural history)
- What is the biggest risk or most common side effect of the proposed treatment?
- What does success look like, and how will we measure it?
- What should prompt me to call before the next scheduled visit?

**If time allows:**
- Lifestyle, diet, or activity interactions with the condition or treatment
- How long before I should expect to notice a change?
- Are there any alternatives we haven't discussed?

**Write them down and rank them.** If you run out of time, the most important ones get answered first.

---

### What to Bring

- **Medication list** — all prescriptions, OTC drugs, vitamins, and supplements with doses and frequency
- **Symptom log** — dates, severity on a 1–10 scale, triggers, what helped or didn't
- **Prior records relevant to this issue** — previous test results, imaging reports, specialist notes
- **A second person if possible** — they'll remember things you won't, ask questions you're too anxious to raise, and can advocate if things move quickly or you're in distress
- **Pen and paper or phone for notes** — even if you think you'll remember, you won't

---

### Framing Research You've Done

If you've done research before the appointment, present it as an invitation to collaborate rather than a challenge:

**Instead of:** "I read that Drug X is better than what you prescribed."

**Try:** "I came across a comparison between Drug X and what you've recommended. I'd love your perspective on whether that's relevant to my situation."

This positions you as engaged and informed, not combative. Most physicians respond well to patients who bring questions rather than conclusions.

---

## During the Appointment

### The Teach-Back Method

After the doctor explains something important, confirm your understanding by restating it in your own words:

> "So if I understand correctly, what you're saying is [your restatement] — is that right?"

This catches misunderstandings in the room rather than later at home. It signals you're engaged and taking this seriously. Physicians generally appreciate it.

---

### If You Don't Understand

Say so explicitly. This is not weakness — unclear communication between clinicians and patients is a documented patient safety issue.

> "Can you explain that in plainer language?"
> "What would that actually look like for me day-to-day?"
> "What's the realistic worst case if I don't pursue this treatment?"
> "Can you write that down for me?"

---

### Taking Notes

Record (or ask permission to record):
- The exact diagnosis — name, stage or severity classification
- Recommended treatment — name, dose, duration, mechanism in plain language
- What you're watching for: signs it's working, and red flags that should prompt a call
- Next steps and follow-up timeline

If the appointment moves too fast: "I want to make sure I have this right — can we slow down for a moment?"

---

## After the Appointment

### Consolidating the Plan

- Review your notes the same day while memory is fresh
- Look up any terms, drug names, or procedures you didn't fully understand
- If instructions feel incomplete or contradictory, call the office — nurse lines exist precisely for this

### Following Up on Unanswered Questions

If you ran out of time on important questions:
- Call the office and ask if the nurse can relay a specific question to the physician
- Many practices have patient portals where you can send messages directly
- Save remaining questions for the follow-up appointment and note them now

---

## When to Seek a Second Opinion

Second opinions are normal, expected, and not disrespectful. They're warranted when:

- The diagnosis is serious (cancer, major surgery, complex chronic condition)
- The recommended treatment is irreversible or carries significant risk
- You feel uncertain about the diagnosis or feel unheard
- Treatment isn't working as expected after a reasonable trial
- You want confirmation before a major decision

**How to ask your current doctor (if you choose to):**
> "I'd like to get a second opinion before moving forward — I want to feel fully confident in the path."

Most experienced physicians welcome this. A doctor who discourages second opinions is a yellow flag worth noting.

**You don't have to tell them at all.** Just schedule one. You can simply request your records be sent to another physician.

---

## Special Context: Using AI-Assisted Research at an Appointment

If you've used an AI (like this session) to research your condition:

- Treat the output as preparation material, not clinical conclusions
- Use it to generate better questions, not to argue a diagnosis
- Be transparent if asked: "I did some research and I want to ask about..."
- Remember: AI doesn't have your labs, your physical exam findings, your imaging, or your history — your doctor does

The goal is to arrive as a more informed participant in your own care, not to substitute the AI for the physician.
