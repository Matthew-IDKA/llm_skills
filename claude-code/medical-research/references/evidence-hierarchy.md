# Evidence Hierarchy for Laypeople

## The Pyramid (Highest to Lowest)

### 1. Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses
- **What:** A rigorous pooling of many studies on the same question, with statistical synthesis
- **Where:** Cochrane Library (cochrane.org), PubMed with `systematic review[pt]` filter
- **Caveats:** Only as good as the underlying studies; check that it's recent (medicine evolves)

### 2. Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs)
- **What:** Participants randomly assigned to treatment vs. control group — the gold standard for causation
- **Where:** PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov results
- **Caveats:**
  - Sample size matters — small RCTs can be underpowered and misleading
  - Industry-sponsored RCTs may have selective outcome reporting
  - Surrogate endpoints (e.g., "improved a blood marker") ≠ clinical outcomes (e.g., "lived longer")

### 3. Observational Studies (Cohort, Case-Control)
- **What:** Researchers observe what happens to people with different exposures — no random assignment
- **Can show:** Association between factors and outcomes
- **Cannot show:** Causation (confounding is always possible)
- **Classic trap:** "People who drink red wine have lower heart disease rates" — they also tend to have higher income and better overall diets

### 4. Case Reports and Case Series
- **What:** One or a few patients with an unusual presentation or outcome
- **Value:** Hypothesis-generating; useful for rare conditions where RCTs can't exist
- **Limitation:** No comparison group; massive selection bias

### 5. Expert Opinion and Consensus Guidelines
- **What:** Professional body recommendations (AHA, USPSTF, NICE)
- **Value:** Synthesizes evidence for clinical practice; useful for "what is standard of care"
- **Caveats:** Guidelines lag evidence by years; committee members can have conflicts of interest

---

## Red Flags — Treat With Extreme Skepticism

- **Press releases** about unpublished research ("study shows X" with no citation)
- **Supplement vendor content** — almost always cherry-picked or misrepresented
- **Testimonials and anecdotes** — powerful psychologically, low evidential value
- **Studies with n < 30** for continuous outcomes
- **"Promising early results"** — most promising early results don't replicate
- **No peer review** — preprints (medRxiv, bioRxiv) are unreviewed drafts
- **Conflicts of interest undisclosed** — always check the funding/disclosure section

## How to Evaluate a Specific Study

Ask these questions in order:

1. **What type of study is it?** (see hierarchy above — this sets the ceiling on what it can claim)
2. **How large was the sample?** (larger is generally more reliable for continuous outcomes)
3. **Who funded it?** (industry funding isn't disqualifying, but it's a flag to look harder at methodology)
4. **Was it published in a peer-reviewed journal?** (predatory journals exist — check Beall's list or journal impact factor)
5. **Has it been replicated?** (a single study is a data point; replication is evidence)
6. **What were the outcomes?** (clinical endpoints like mortality vs. surrogate markers like a lab value)
7. **What is the absolute effect size?** (not just the relative reduction — see statistics primer)

## The Cochrane Library — Special Mention

Cochrane reviews are widely considered the gold standard for evidence synthesis. They:
- Follow a rigorous pre-specified protocol to minimize bias
- Actively seek unpublished data and negative studies
- Rate the quality of underlying evidence using the GRADE system
- Are freely accessible at **cochrane.org**

If a Cochrane review exists for your question, start there before anything else.

---

## Understanding GRADE Evidence Quality

Many guidelines and Cochrane reviews use the GRADE system to rate evidence quality:

| Grade | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **High** | Very confident the true effect is close to the estimate |
| **Moderate** | Moderately confident; the true effect is probably close but may differ |
| **Low** | Limited confidence; the true effect may be substantially different |
| **Very Low** | Very little confidence; the true effect is likely different from the estimate |

A "strong recommendation" based on "low quality evidence" is a signal to probe further.
