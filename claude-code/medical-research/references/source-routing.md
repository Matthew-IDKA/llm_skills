# Trusted Sources by Type and Condition

## General Purpose — Start Here

| Source | Best For | Trust Level |
|---|---|---|
| NIH MedlinePlus (medlineplus.gov) | Plain-language condition and drug overviews | High — NIH authored |
| Mayo Clinic (mayoclinic.org) | Comprehensive condition overviews, conservative and well-sourced | High |
| CDC (cdc.gov) | Infectious disease, vaccines, epidemiology, public health data | High |
| AHRQ Patient Guides (ahrq.gov) | Evidence summaries written specifically for patients | High |
| UpToDate (uptodate.com) | Clinical detail; some free patient-facing summary pages | High — written for clinicians |

---

## Evidence Synthesis — For Digging Deeper

| Source | Best For |
|---|---|
| Cochrane Library (cochrane.org) | Systematic reviews — the highest quality evidence synthesis available |
| PubMed / PMC (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov) | Primary literature search; free full text via PMC |
| USPSTF (uspreventiveservicestaskforce.org) | Preventive care recommendations with explicit evidence grades |

**PubMed search tips:**
- Add `systematic review[pt]` or `meta-analysis[pt]` to filter by study type
- Add `free full text[sb]` to limit to open-access articles
- Add a date filter (e.g., `2020:2026[dp]`) for fast-moving fields
- Search for condition + drug name + "randomized" for RCTs

## Clinical Guidelines (Standard of Care by Domain)

| Organization | Domain |
|---|---|
| ACC / AHA | Cardiology |
| NCCN (nccn.org) | Cancer — free patient guidelines available |
| ADA (diabetes.org) | Diabetes |
| NIDDK (niddk.nih.gov) | Kidney, diabetes, digestive diseases |
| NICE (nice.org.uk) | UK guidelines — often more conservative and evidence-strict than US equivalents |
| AAP (aap.org) | Pediatrics |
| ACOG (acog.org) | Obstetrics and gynecology |
| ACR (rheumatology.org) | Rheumatology, arthritis, autoimmune |
| AAN (aan.com) | Neurology |
| IDSA (idsociety.org) | Infectious disease |

**On guidelines:** They represent professional consensus, not binding rules. They can lag evidence by years. Your doctor may deviate from guidelines for a good reason — it is completely appropriate to ask why.

| Source | What It Offers |
|---|---|
| The NNT (thennt.com) | Pre-computed NNTs and NNHs for common treatments — essential for translating statistics |
| Science-Based Medicine (sciencebasedmedicine.org) | Rigorous appraisal of health claims; especially useful for evaluating supplements and alternative treatments |
| Sensible Medicine (sensible-medicine.org) | Physician-written skeptical appraisal, particularly strong on cardiology and overdiagnosis |
| Less Wrong / Astral Codex Ten | Not medical specifically, but useful for calibrating probabilistic reasoning and Bayesian thinking about evidence |

## Sources to Approach Carefully

These are not useless, but require more skepticism and cross-referencing:

- **WebMD / Healthline:** Generally accurate for basics but engagement-optimized — may overstate risk or treatment benefits
- **Wikipedia medical articles:** Variable quality; useful as an entry point and map of terminology, not a primary source
- **Disease Facebook groups / Reddit communities (e.g., r/ChronicIllness):** Valuable for patient experience, lived reality, and practical tips; poor for evaluating evidence — treat anecdotes as anecdotes
- **News articles about new research:** Almost always based on press releases; rarely capture the study's caveats or limitations; always locate the underlying study

---

## Red Flag Sources — Do Not Cite as Evidence

- Any site selling a product related to the condition
- Wellness influencer content citing "studies" without direct links
- Testimonial-based sites ("This cured my X!")
- Sites claiming a single treatment or compound cures many unrelated conditions
- News headlines alone — always read the actual study abstract at minimum

---

## When a Condition Is Rare

For rare or poorly-studied conditions:

- Patient advocacy foundations often maintain the best synthesis of current research (e.g., NORD for rare diseases at rarediseases.org)
- Academic medical centers with specialty programs often publish patient-facing materials
- ClinicalTrials.gov — find active or completed trials; useful for understanding what's being studied and whether a trial might be relevant
- Patient registries and natural history studies may be the primary evidence base — acknowledge this uncertainty explicitly

---

## A Note on "Dr. Google"

Searching symptoms without context almost always returns worst-case scenarios. This is not because the sources are wrong — it's because serious conditions are disproportionately represented in medical literature relative to their actual prevalence.

Use search to understand a condition you've already been told you have, or to formulate better questions. Do not use search to self-diagnose from symptoms.
